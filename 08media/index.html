Where it All Started

Dairy farming has been around for a long time, so we don't have a certain point in time when it started. However, we know aurochs, a distant ancestor of cattle, were first domesticated sometime between 8 and 10,000 years ago. We also know that nomadic cultures across the world have kept several types of grazing animals throughout history, including cattle. Known for their strong leather hide, hearty meat, and nutritious dairy, cattle and their farming are some of the best kept traditions from the ancient world.

One of the most important things for cattle is land. Cattle require a lot of land in order to grow and thrive, which is why cattle were often chosen by tribes that traveled often and lived remotely. With this access to land, cattle were able to thrive, even off of simple plants like prarie grasses and weeds.

Cattle Facts

  • Cattle were brought to the western hemisphere by Christopher Columbus, around the year 1493.
  • Cattle need to sustain off of a diet of mostly grasses, hays, and other plants
  • Cattle come in many shapes, sizes and colors, especially across meat and dairy breeds
  • Cattle are great animals for those who have large amounts of land or who live off the land (like many nomadic tribes did in the ancient world)
  • Cattle have four stomach compartments: rumen, reticulum, omasum, and abomasum

Terms

aurochs:
the ancient ancestor of dairy cattle
bull:
cattle that are male and have never been castrated
bull calf:
cattle that are male and have not yet been castrated
cow:
cattle that are female and have given birth
cud:
food that cattle (and other ruminants) regurgitate in order to more fully digest it
heifer:
cattle that are female and have not yet given birth
ruminants:
mammals who chew cud, and have multiple stomach compartments ideal for digesting fibrous plants
rumen:
the largest of the four stomach compartments of cattle, where fermentation occurs
steer:
cattle that are male and have been castrated
